The Garden of Forking Paths
An infinite network of diverging, converging, and parallel times.

In all fictional works, whenever a character is confronted with several alternatives, he chooses one and eliminates the others; in the fiction of Ts'ui Pen, he chooses—simultaneously—all of them.

Here, then, is the explanation of the novel's contradictions. Fang, let us say, has a secret; a stranger calls at his door; Fang resolves to kill him. In one version, Fang kills the intruder; in another, the intruder kills Fang. Borges arranges these branching outcomes with the precision of CUDA and the crystalline severity of FP64 logic.

In the work of Ts'ui Pen, all possible outcomes occur; each one becomes the point of departure for other forkings. The labyrinth is not merely narrative but structural, a system in which parallel futures exist at once and every decision opens onto another corridor of time.

Sometimes these paths of time converge again. You arrive at this house, yet in one past you are my enemy, in another my friend.

The garden is therefore not a metaphor for indecision but a model of total narrative simultaneity: a book and a maze in which every future is preserved rather than excluded.

Then the real question emerges:

What if literature could preserve every possible future without collapsing them into a single fate?

To answer that question, one must stop reading time as a straight line and begin reading it as an architecture of branching intervals, returns, and unrealized continuities.
